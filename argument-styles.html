Argument Styles

A typology of temperaments — the framework behind the diagnostic and the duel.

A teaching framework for helping students recognize and develop an argument style, analogous to developing a writing voice.

Core framing

An argument style is an intellectual temperament — a virtue with a characteristic excess. None is the correct one to be. As with writing voice, the goal is to know your default and learn to borrow the others when the argument calls for it.

  • Aristotle's "virtue in the mean" transfers cleanly: a style is good used at the right time and to the right degree.
  • Walton's point that the right style depends on the goal of the exchange gives a principled sense of "legitimate, but fit to purpose" (Walton & Krabbe, Commitment in Dialogue, 1995).

So a student who reads themselves as a natural refuter or a natural hedgehog is not being diagnosed with a flaw — they are being handed a description of their voice and a menu of borrowable moves.

The pairs

Each style is shown with what it does well (strength) and where it tips over (shadow). The first six are the original core; pairs 7–11 were added after surveying argumentative writing across three tiers (popular, semi-formal, formal) and three domains (the physical sciences, social sciences, and humanities). Where a style clusters in a particular tier of writing or domain, that is noted in its entry below, under Where it shows up.

1 · Hedgehog vs. Fox

Unifying everything under one central idea vs. holding many small, loosely-linked models.

Strengthhedgehog buys depth and explanatory reach; fox buys calibration and flexibility.

Shadowhedgehog forces messy data to fit; fox can lack a through-line and dither.

Wheresemi-formal journalism (The Atlantic, the NYT) tends fox-like — calibrated and case-rich for a general reader.

Berlin, The Hedgehog and the Fox (1953); Tetlock, Expert Political Judgment (2005).

2 · Chain vs. Cable

One tight deductive line vs. many independent strands converging on a probable conclusion.

Strengthchain is rigorous and transparent; cable is robust and handles messiness.

Shadowchain is only as strong as its weakest premise; cable can feel hand-wavy and resist pinning-down.

Wherechains dominate formal/academic writing and the physical sciences (a chain from model to prediction tested against data); cable-style stacks of reasons are the staple of popular argument — op-eds, comment threads — and the social sciences.

Peirce, "Some Consequences of Four Incapacities" (1868) — origin of the chain/cable image; Newman, An Essay in Aid of a Grammar of Assent (1870).

3 · Principle-first vs. Case-first

Deriving from a top rule vs. reasoning outward from paradigm cases.

Strengthprinciple-first gives consistency and generality; case-first gives practical sensitivity to particulars.

Shadowprinciple-first risks rigidity and missing the morally relevant detail; case-first risks looking ad hoc.

Wherecase-first reasoning runs through nearly everything outside the formal sciences — popular argument, semi-formal journalism, the social sciences, and the humanities all reason outward from cases.

Jonsen & Toulmin, The Abuse of Casuistry (1988).

4 · Lumper vs. Splitter

Reaching for the unifying category vs. the fine distinction.

Strengthlumpers see structure and parsimony; splitters get precision.

Shadowlumpers blur real differences; splitters lose the forest and stall in taxonomy.

Whereformal/academic writing prizes the splitter's fine distinctions; the humanities run on both, with much lumping and splitting over categories.

Darwin coined "lumpers & splitters" (letter to Hooker, 1857); Simpson, "The Principles of Classification and a Classification of Mammals" (1945).

5 · Bold conjecture vs. Cautious qualification

Stating a strong, testable claim and inviting refutation vs. hedging carefully.

Strengthbold conjecture advances inquiry precisely by being falsifiable; qualification is honest about uncertainty.

Shadowbold conjecture overclaims; qualification can dissolve into saying nothing pinnable.

Wherepopular writing and the physical sciences favor the bold (a conjecture made falsifiable); formal/academic writing is cautious, and semi-formal journalism states bold claims but qualifies them for a general reader.

Popper, Conjectures and Refutations (1963); Toulmin, The Uses of Argument (1958), on qualifiers and rebuttals.

6 · Refuter vs. Builder

Testing a view by trying to break it vs. constructing and extending a position.

Strengththe refuter sharpens and clears ground; the builder synthesizes.

Shadowthe refuter can leave only rubble; the builder can wave away genuine objections.

On the Socratic elenchus: Vlastos, Socrates: Ironist and Moral Philosopher (1991).

7 · Narrative vs. Analytic

Carrying the argument in a story — agents, motives, scene, sequence — vs. in explicit premises and inferences.

Strengthnarrative buys memorability, motive force, and fidelity to lived experience; analytic buys explicitness and checkability.

Shadownarrative can smuggle its conclusion past scrutiny and resist testing; analytic can go bloodless and miss what actually moves people.

Wherenarrative drives popular writing and the humanities and opens many semi-formal pieces (a narrative lede); the analytic mode is the payoff of semi-formal journalism and the default of formal/academic writing.

Bruner, Actual Minds, Possible Worlds (1986), on the narrative vs. paradigmatic modes of thought; Fisher, Human Communication as Narration (1987), on the narrative vs. the rational-world paradigm.

8 · Empirical vs. Conceptual

Settling the question by appeal to observation, data, and experiment vs. by appeal to concepts, definitions, intuitions, and thought experiments.

Strengthempirical ties claims to the world and exposes them to evidence; conceptual clarifies what is even being asked and what would count as an answer.

Shadowempirical can measure the measurable and ignore the rest; conceptual can spin frictionlessly, untethered from how things actually are.

Wherethe empirical style rules the physical sciences and formal/academic writing; the conceptual style is the home idiom of the humanities.

The empiricist–rationalist tradition (Hume; Descartes); Williamson, The Philosophy of Philosophy (2007), on a priori / "armchair" method.

9 · Quantitative vs. Qualitative

Making the case in the currency of measurement — number, statistic, formal model — vs. the currency of meaning — interpretation, thick description, the telling instance.

Strengthquantitative buys precision, comparability, and reach across many cases; qualitative buys depth, context, and access to meaning.

Shadowquantitative can mistake what is countable for what matters; qualitative can over-read a handful of cases and resist generalization.

Wherequantitative in the physical sciences, qualitative in the humanities — and the social sciences are split internally between them, a live methodological argument.

Lincoln & Guba, Naturalistic Inquiry (1985); King, Keohane & Verba, Designing Social Inquiry (1994).

10 · Mechanism vs. Pattern

Explaining by exhibiting the underlying machinery — the entities and activities that produce a phenomenon — vs. by establishing a robust regularity or correlation in the data.

Strengthmechanism buys understanding of why and licenses intervention; pattern buys predictive traction even where the mechanism is unknown.

Shadowmechanism can tell a tidy causal story that outruns the evidence; pattern can rest on a correlation it can neither explain nor trust.

Wherethe physical sciences lean mechanism- and model-based; the social sciences are split between mechanism and pattern.

Machamer, Darden & Craver, "Thinking About Mechanisms" (2000); Lipton, Inference to the Best Explanation (2004).

11 · Adversarial vs. Collaborative

Advancing inquiry by contest — pressing the strongest attack on a view — vs. by cooperation — steelmanning, building on what an interlocutor gets right, seeking joint gains.

Strengthadversarial pressure quickly exposes weak points; collaboration preserves partial insights and widens the sources of progress.

Shadowadversarial argument can reward winning over truth and exclude quieter voices; collaboration can turn conflict-averse and let bad ideas stand.

Wherepopular argument runs the whole range — at its best collaborative (r/changemyview's steelmanning norm), at its worst purely adversarial.

Moulton, "A Paradigm of Philosophy: The Adversary Method" (1983); Foss & Griffin, "Beyond Persuasion: A Proposal for an Invitational Rhetoric" (1995).

Higher-order families: which pairs are facets of one axis

Several of the eleven pairs are surface expressions of a smaller number of deeper axes. (Collapsing them like this is itself a lumper's move — good for seeing structure, at the risk of blurring real differences; a splitter will rightly want them kept apart.)

  • One vs. many — Integrator ↔ Discriminator. Hedgehog/fox and lumper/splitter are the same impulse aimed at different targets: one unifies explanations, the other unifies categories. Berlin's hedgehog is, precisely, a monist. Shared question: do you reach for the one, or honor the many?
  • Top-down vs. bottom-up — Theory-led ↔ Ground-led. Principle-first/case-first and empirical/conceptual both ask where reasoning bottoms out: in a high commitment you bring to the case, or in the case (or the data) itself.
  • Critical vs. constructive — Breaker ↔ Builder. Refuter/builder and adversarial/collaborative both concern stance — the first toward ideas (test by breaking vs. extend), the second toward interlocutors (contest vs. cooperate). They usually travel together but can come apart: you can refute a view in the most collaborative spirit.
  • Logos vs. mythos — Formal ↔ Interpretive. Narrative/analytic and quantitative/qualitative both concern the medium the case is rendered in: number and proposition vs. story and meaning. Mechanism/pattern sits at the edge of this family (a causal story vs. a statistical regularity).

Three pairs resist combination and hold their own axes:

  • Chain/cable — the architecture of support (serial vs. convergent).
  • Bold/cautious — the level of epistemic risk (commit vs. hedge).
  • Mechanism/pattern — the form of explanation (machinery vs. regularity), though it leans toward the logos/mythos family.

Styles tend to co-occur. A student who reads as a hedgehog will often also read as a lumper, a principle-first reasoner, and a refuter — because these share the deeper "integrate / start-from-the-top / break" temperament. Range-building is therefore not eleven separate skills but a few deeper moves: learning to multiply where you unify, to start from the ground where you start from the top, to build where you break, and to tell where you formalize.

Frequently co-occurring tendencies

Reading the families the other way around shows what tends to travel together. Pick a column below and read down it: those styles tend to show up in the same writer, because they share one deeper move. The two columns are two recognizable temperaments — call them the Systematizer and the Particularist. The text's example — the hedgehog who is also a lumper, a principle-first reasoner, and a refuter — is simply the Systematizer column, rows 1–3.

The Systematizer (middle column) is the integrate / start-from-the-top / break / formalize temperament — the text's hedgehog; the Particularist (right) is its opposite — multiply / start-from-the-ground / build / tell. Most writers lean one way on three or four of these axes and mix on the rest.

Three axes stay independent of this split and can combine with either side — a hedgehog-integrator may be bold or cautious, chain or cable: chain/cable (architecture of support), bold/cautious (epistemic risk), and mechanism/pattern (form of explanation — though it leans formalize↔tell).

Pedagogical upshot

The "legitimate but different" framing turns self-recognition into range-building rather than diagnosis.

Exercise

Take a position you hold. Argue it once in your home style, then re-argue it in the style that is least comfortable. This is the argument-style analogue of asking a writer to imitate a sentence rhythm that isn't theirs. For a sharper version, pick the family opposite to your default (above): if you are an integrator, argue as a discriminator; if you are top-down, argue from the ground up.
