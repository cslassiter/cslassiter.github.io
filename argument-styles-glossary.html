Toolkit Glossary

Plain-language definitions of the philosophical concepts behind the eleven argument styles.

These are the concepts named in the philosophical toolkit table — many of them tools from Baggini & Fosl's The Philosopher's Toolkit. The concept terms in that table link straight to the matching entry below.

Monism vs. pluralism (Hedgehog/Fox)
A monist thinks one big idea can explain most of what matters; a pluralist thinks reality needs many separate explanations that don't reduce to one. The hedgehog is a monist by temperament; the fox, a pluralist.
Parsimony / Ockham's razor (Hedgehog/Fox, Lumper/Splitter)
Other things equal, prefer the simpler explanation — don't invent more moving parts than you need. The splitter's worry is that "simpler" can mean "ignoring real differences."
Deduction vs. induction (Chain/Cable)
A deductive argument, if its premises are true, guarantees its conclusion — but one false premise breaks it (the chain). An inductive argument makes its conclusion likely by piling up evidence; no single strand carries it (the cable).
Defeasible reasoning (Chain/Cable)
A defeasible conclusion is one you accept now but would give up if new evidence came in. Cable-style arguments are defeasible by design — a feature, not a weakness.
Generalism vs. particularism (Principle/Case)
A generalist thinks good reasoning starts from general rules and applies them to cases. A particularist thinks the right call can depend so much on the details that no rule captures it — you have to read the case itself.
Reflective equilibrium (Principle/Case)
Going back and forth between your general principles and your gut judgments about specific cases, adjusting each until they fit. It's how principle-first and case-first reasoners can meet in the middle.
Carving nature at its joints / family resemblance (Lumper/Splitter)
Plato's image: a good category cuts reality where it really divides, like a butcher finding the joint. Wittgenstein's rival idea: some categories (like "game") have no single defining feature — members just share overlapping family resemblances. Lumpers trust the joints; splitters doubt they are joints.
The sorites ("heap") problem (Lumper/Splitter)
One grain isn't a heap; adding one grain never seems to make a heap — yet somehow you get a heap. It shows how hard it is to say exactly where a vague category begins.
Falsifiability (Bold/Cautious)
A claim is falsifiable if there's some possible observation that would prove it wrong. Popper's point: a bold, falsifiable claim is valuable because it sticks its neck out and can be tested.
Believe-truth vs. shun-error (Bold/Cautious)
William James named two goals that pull apart: get to the truth and avoid believing falsehoods. Chase truth too hard and you'll believe rashly; avoid error too hard and you'll commit to nothing. Bold vs. cautious is which you weight.
Elenchus, counterexample, reductio (Refuter/Builder)
Three ways to test a claim by attacking it: the elenchus is Socrates' cross-examining until a belief contradicts itself; a counterexample is a single case that breaks a generalization; a reductio assumes a claim and derives an absurdity from it.
Abduction / inference to the best explanation (Refuter/Builder, Mechanism/Pattern)
Reasoning from a puzzling fact to the hypothesis that would best explain it ("the best account of these tracks is that a deer passed"). The builder's engine — and the mechanism-hunter's.
Verstehen vs. Erklären (Narrative/Analytic)
Two German words for two kinds of grasp: Erklären is to explain from the outside by causes and laws; Verstehen is to understand from the inside, by reconstructing what something means to the people involved. Analytic argument aims at the first; narrative at the second.
Knowledge by acquaintance vs. by description (Narrative/Analytic)
Russell's distinction: you can know of something by direct experience (acquaintance — what red looks like) or only through a description (knowing that 700nm light is "red"). Stories trade in acquaintance; analysis in description.
A priori vs. a posteriori (Empirical/Conceptual)
A priori knowledge can be had by thinking alone, before experience (2+2=4); a posteriori knowledge requires observing the world (water boils at 100°C). The empirical style leans a posteriori, the conceptual style a priori.
Hume's fork & the analytic/synthetic distinction (Empirical/Conceptual)
Hume split claims into relations of ideas (true by meaning — "all bachelors are unmarried") and matters of fact (true by how the world is). Kant's near-cousin: analytic truths unpack a concept; synthetic truths add information about the world. Quine later argued the line is blurrier than it looks.
Nomothetic vs. idiographic (Quantitative/Qualitative)
Nomothetic inquiry looks for general laws across many cases (the quantitative impulse); idiographic inquiry tries to understand one particular case in its full richness (the qualitative impulse).
Thick vs. thin concepts; thick description (Quantitative/Qualitative)
A thin concept is bare and general ("good," "large"); a thick concept fuses description and evaluation ("cruel," "courageous"). Thick description (Geertz) reports a scene with enough meaning-laden detail that you grasp what it meant to the people in it.
Causation vs. constant conjunction (Mechanism/Pattern)
Hume noted we never actually see one thing make another happen — only that they regularly occur together (constant conjunction). The pattern-thinker is content with the regularity; the mechanism-thinker insists there's hidden machinery doing the making.
Covering-law vs. mechanistic explanation (Mechanism/Pattern)
One view says you've explained an event once you've shown it follows from a general law plus the conditions (the covering-law model); the rival says real explanation means displaying the mechanism — the parts and activities that produce it.
Underdetermination (Mechanism/Pattern)
When the same evidence is equally compatible with several explanations, the evidence underdetermines the theory. It's why a clean pattern can't, by itself, tell you which mechanism is behind it.
Dialectic vs. eristic (Adversarial/Collaborative)
Aristotle's two kinds of argument: dialectic aims to reach the truth together through reasoned exchange; eristic aims only to win. Adversarial argument is healthy when dialectical, toxic when it slides into eristic.
Principle of charity / steelmanning (Adversarial/Collaborative)
Interpret the other person's argument in its strongest, most reasonable form before you respond. It keeps an adversarial style honest and a collaborative style from going mushy — you beat (or join) the best version of the view, not a caricature.
